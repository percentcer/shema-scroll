1  TL;DR
	 
One-liner
	A Duolingo-style app that helps kids feel genuinely ready for their B'Mitzvah — by learning the sounds and meaning of the blessings in short, fun sessions. Not memorization. Understanding.
	 
The problem
	Thousands of kids approach their B'Mitzvah anxious and underprepared — not because they don't care, but because existing options are too rigid (Hebrew school), too expensive (private tutors), or too shallow (YouTube). Many families — especially unaffiliated or interfaith ones — have no clear path.
45% of families whose 12-13 year old didn't have a B'Mitzvah cite child resistance as the primary reason. Much of that resistance is anxiety masquerading as disinterest. The kid doesn't know the words, doesn't understand what they mean, and doesn't feel ready.
	 
The solution
	Short, accessible practice sessions — hear the blessing, follow along with transliteration, learn what it actually means in plain English. No Hebrew background required. No synagogue membership. No assumed knowledge.
Built for kids, but designed so a parent who was never b'mitzvahed — or isn't Jewish — can sit alongside and actually understand what their kid is learning.
	 
Sprint deliverable
	Proof of concept: a working flow for one core blessing (the Shema) — hear it, follow along, understand what it means. Simple, demonstrable, and enough for CE to react to. Full gamification, multiple blessings, and progress tracking are nice-to-haves depending on build time.
	 
2  CE MISSION FIT
	 
Who it serves
	Kids 10-13 approaching a B'Mitzvah — especially those in unaffiliated, cultural, or interfaith families who aren't in Hebrew school, can't afford regular tutoring, or whose child is resistant to traditional prep.
Parents are the secondary user: particularly those who weren't b'mitzvahed themselves, aren't Jewish, or simply don't know the blessings well enough to help. The app should work for the whole family, not just the kid.
	 
Why CE can do this
	The B'Mitzvah prep market is dominated by expensive tutors and synagogue programs that require membership and prior affiliation. There's nothing accessible, modern, and genuinely non-denominational that works for the families CE is trying to reach.
CE is uniquely positioned to build this without the institutional baggage — no synagogue affiliation, no assumed Hebrew knowledge, no judgment about how Jewish you are or aren't.
	 
Jewish lens
	Meaning-first. The app doesn't treat Hebrew as a performance requirement — it treats it as something interesting. Every blessing has a story, a reason, and a human moment behind it. That's the hook for secular and unaffiliated families.
Non-denominational by design. Works for Reform, Conservative, Reconstructionist, unaffiliated, interfaith, and 'JewISH' families. B'Mitzvah styles vary wildly — some families want full Hebrew chanting within a long service, others just want to say the Shema together at a backyard party. This app serves both without assuming.

Sprint MVP
	Shema Yisrael — short, singable, the most recognizable moment in Jewish life. Audio playback + transliteration so anyone can follow, no Hebrew reading required.

NICE TO HAVE — Your Torah Portion
Torah Portion
	A lightweight discovery feature, distinct from blessing practice. Enter your B'Mitzvah date and the app shows you: which Torah portion is yours, a plain-English summary of what's in it, and a brief explanation of how the weekly parasha cycle works — why this portion falls on your date, and what it means that it's 'yours.'
Informational, not instructional — no trope, no chanting, no attempt to teach the full portion. Think of it as a personalized context card: 'Your portion is Lech-Lecha. Here's what happens in it, and why every B'Mitzvah kid has their own.'

6  CORE FLOW
	 
SPRINT SCOPE · Build steps 1–3 as the proof-of-concept core. Steps 4–5 are nice to have.
The goal is for CE to experience the loop firsthand: land → hear the Shema → understand what it means. That alone is enough to react to.
	 
1
	Landing
	"Feel ready for your B'Mitzvah." — The anxiety is the entry point. The promise is confidence, not curriculum.
"Learn the blessings. Understand what they mean. Feel ready." Simple, direct, non-intimidating.
CTA: 'Start with the Shema' — no login required, no setup. Just begin
Note: sample copy; feel free to edit or adjust. 
	2
	The Shema session
	(a) Hear it — audio playback of the full Shema with a prominent play button. Big text. No assumed Hebrew literacy.
(b) Follow along — transliteration line by line so anyone can read along out loud, even with zero Hebrew background.
(c) What does it mean? — 3-4 short paragraphs in plain English. Not a lecture. More like: 'here's the thing Jews say, here's why, here's what it actually means word by word.'
Tone: curious and warm, like a smart older sibling explaining — not a rabbi, not a textbook.
	3
	End of session
	Short, encouraging close: 'You just learned the Shema. That's the most important moment in Jewish life — and now you know what it means.'
Optional: simple 3-question quiz mixing sound recognition and meaning. Not required for the sprint build.
Clear next step: 'Up next: Kiddush — the blessing over wine' (if built). Or just: 'Come back tomorrow for the next one.'
	4
	Progress (nice to have)
	Confidence bar that fills as the kid completes sessions — visual, tangible, satisfying.
B'Mitzvah countdown timer: 'Your B'Mitzvah is in 127 days.'
Encouraging feedback after quiz: specific, not just a score ('You know the sounds — work on the last line').
	5
	Shareable moment (nice to have)
	'You know the Shema. ✓' — simple unlock moment when a blessing is completed. Earned, not just checked off.
Optional parent view: what did my kid just learn? One-screen summary, readable by a non-Hebrew speaker.
Optional link to B'Mitzvah 2.0: 'Design your whole B'Mitzvah journey →'
	 
7  AI & CONTENT DIRECTION
	 
AI SHOULD
	AI SHOULD NOT
	Generate the meaning explanation for each prayer in plain, conversational English — no jargon, no assumed knowledge
	Generate Hebrew audio (use licensed recordings for accuracy — mispronounced AI audio is worse than no audio)
	Adapt quiz questions based on which answers the kid keeps getting wrong — more reps on the hard parts
	Evaluate pronunciation in MVP — technically complex and error-prone; keep it tap-to-listen for now
	Power a "Did you know?" fact for each prayer — short, interesting, shareable cultural context
	Replace human tutors — the app supplements; for complex Torah portion practice, always recommend a real teacher
	 
8  VISUAL VIBE
	 
Tone
	Encouraging · Gamified · Warm. The app should feel like an achievement, not an obligation. Every session ends with the kid feeling better than when they started.
	 
Reference points
	Duolingo (gamified progress, streaks, celebratory animations) meets something a Jewish 12-year-old would actually want to open. Warm greens and golds — not institutional blue. Progress feels earned.
	 
Visual guidelines
	Progress bar (or similar visual sign of progress) fills up as confidence grows — satisfying, visual, tangible. Never show a 'score'; show a 'confidence level.'
Hebrew letters rendered clearly and beautifully — big, confident, not squeezed into a corner. Make the script feel interesting, not intimidating.
Audio play button is prominent and always visible.
Illustration style: modern, warm, non-stereotypical. Diverse kids. 
Unlock fun graphics or animations when a module is completed — brief, satisfying, earned.
	 
Comparable products
	Duolingo (structure, gamification, streak mechanic), Reading.com reading app (Great simple learning process and fun gamification), Spotify (the app you actually want to open).
